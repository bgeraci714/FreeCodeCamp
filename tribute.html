Grace Murray Hopper

"The First Lady of Software"


Grace Hopper was a remarkable woman who rose to the challenges of early programming with the first available computers. She was known for inventing the first compiler for a programming language. She was also responsible for popularizing the idea of machine-independent programming, which eventually lead to the inception of COBOL. She is also noted for one of the first instances of "debugging" after finding a moth stuck in a relay of the Mark II computer.

  • 1906: Born on December 9, 1906 in New York City, NY.
  • 1928: Earned her Bachelor's degree in mathematics and physics from Vassar College.
  • 1930: Earned her master's degree at Yale University.
  • 1934: Earned her Ph.D. in mathematics from Yale. Her dissertation was titled "New Types of Irreducibility Criteria" and published that same year.
  • 1931: Hopper began teaching mathematics at Vassar College.
  • 1941: Hopper was promoted to associate professor.
  • 1943: Hopper left Vassar College in order to join the United States Navy Reserve, and ultimately served in WAVES (Women Accepted for Volunteer Emergency Service).
  • 1944: Assigned to the Bureau of Ships Computation Project at Harvard University.
  • 1949: hopper became an employee at Eckert-Mauchly Computer Corporation and began work on the UNIVAC I.
  • 1952: Hopper gets her compiler operational!
  • 1959: Began working on COBOL (COmmon Business-Oriented Language).
  • 1973: Hopper is promoted to the rank of Captain.
  • 1992: Passes away on January 1st, 1992 at the age of 85. She would be buried in Arlington Virginia at the rank of Rear Admiral.
